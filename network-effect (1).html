The Network Effect

Why a shared, cross-campus collection of AI resources generates institutional culture—not just an archive. A research-grounded brief on the interdisciplinary benefits of AI resource commons in higher education.

← Back to the Commons

The Case for Shared AI Resource Collections

The rapid adoption of artificial intelligence across higher education has created what researchers describe as a fundamental reconsideration of knowledge construction, academic authority, and educational purpose. AI is now being integrated into teaching, research, and institutional operations across virtually every discipline—but this integration typically happens in isolation, within individual departments or programs, with limited visibility across institutional boundaries.

A growing body of scholarship argues that the greatest benefits of AI in higher education emerge not from any single tool or application, but from the interdisciplinary connections that become possible when AI-related work is made visible across institutional silos. The AI Commons is designed to instantiate precisely this kind of network effect.

The interdisciplinary interest from scholars across linguistics, psychology, education, and neuroscience who connect AI to knowledge in their own disciplines creates both challenges and extraordinary opportunities for cross-pollination.

What the Research Shows

Several interconnected findings from the scholarly literature support the rationale behind a cross-campus AI commons:

1. Interdisciplinarity Drives Innovation

Research consistently demonstrates that interdisciplinary collaboration fosters innovation, facilitates creativity, and supports broader research impact. Newman (2024), studying faculty at a major research university, found that while material barriers impede cross-disciplinary work, the benefits of interdisciplinary collaboration—novel solutions, creative methodologies, and greater societal relevance—are widely acknowledged by both faculty and institutional leaders. The challenge is making interdisciplinary opportunities visible and accessible, which is precisely what a shared resource commons accomplishes.

2. AI Tools Measurably Enhance Interdisciplinary Outcomes

Zhang and Tang (2025), publishing in Scientific Reports, found a 37% increase in interdisciplinary project outcomes when AI-generated content tools were strategically implemented across humanities, STEM, and social science programs. Their mixed-methods study across multiple institutions demonstrated that AI tools showed substantial value in breaking down disciplinary silos, with significant variations in effectiveness based on implementation approach. A shared commons helps faculty discover effective implementation patterns from other disciplines.

3. AI Research Is Inherently Cross-Disciplinary

Crompton and Burke (2023), in their systematic review published in the International Journal of Educational Technology in Higher Education, mapped the state of AI applications across all of higher education. They found that AI in education draws foundational contributions from computer science, management information systems, education, and psychology simultaneously. The implication is clear: AI resources created in one discipline carry transferable insights for many others. A commons makes these transfers discoverable rather than accidental.

4. Cross-Disciplinary AI Resources Need Shared Infrastructure

Kusters et al. (2020), writing in Frontiers in Big Data, analyzed the challenges and opportunities of interdisciplinary AI research and concluded that future development of AI should not only impact other scientific domains but should also take inspiration from them. They emphasize that combining AI with other fields requires overcoming communication barriers due to differences in terminologies, methods, and cultures—barriers that a curated, well-cataloged commons directly addresses by establishing shared vocabulary and discoverability.

5. Institutional AI Policy Benefits from Transparency

Farhi et al. (2025), in their analysis published in PMC, argue that GenAI requires proactive institutional strategies that balance innovation with integrity, calling for inclusive policies, ethical frameworks, and the development of critical AI literacies. When AI governance documents—from university-wide policies through college-level adaptations to department-specific protocols—are collected in a single discoverable location, governance becomes participatory rather than top-down. Faculty can see how policies cascade and can contribute to their evolution.

6. Shared Resources Strengthen Pedagogical Innovation

Podgórska (2024), studying interdisciplinary collaboration for sustainable development in higher education, found that project-based learning across disciplinary teams is most effective when supported by shared resources and adequate institutional infrastructure. The same principle applies to AI integration: when faculty can see what colleagues in other programs have built, they are more likely to adapt and extend those approaches than to start from scratch. A commons dramatically reduces the activation energy for pedagogical innovation.

7. The Teaching Square Effect: Professional Networks Form Around Shared Visibility

Jackson et al. (2024), studying interdisciplinary teaching squares across nursing, pharmacy, and biomedical science at an Australian university, found that one of the most significant unexpected outcomes was the formation of interdisciplinary professional relationships and networks. When academics could see their peers' teaching practices across disciplinary boundaries, new collaborations emerged organically. A digital commons operates on the same principle at institutional scale—making pedagogical practice visible creates professional community.

8. AI Facilitates Cross-Disciplinary Knowledge Transfer

Drago et al. (2023), surveying Romanian academics across five universities for Societies, found that AI offers exceptional opportunities to increase interdisciplinary, multidisciplinary, and transdisciplinary research by facilitating searching across sources, transferring methods between fields, and mixing research methodologies. A commons of AI-generated resources embodies this principle: a prompt library created for biology can inspire parallel resources in chemistry, nursing, or aviation.

9. Students Benefit from Seeing AI Used Across the Institution

Zawacki-Richter et al. (2019), in their widely-cited systematic review in the International Journal of Educational Technology in Higher Education, highlighted a critical gap: educators are underrepresented in the AI-in-education literature relative to technologists. A cross-campus AI commons recenters faculty expertise by showcasing how educators use AI rather than focusing solely on the tools themselves. When students can see AI being used responsibly across nursing, philosophy, aviation, and criminal justice, the commons becomes a living syllabus of institutional AI literacy.

10. Shared Collections Create Institutional Memory

The UNESCO Guidance for Generative Artificial Intelligence in Education and Research (2024) and the UNESCO Recommendation on Open Science (2021) both emphasize the importance of making research processes and outcomes transparent, accessible, and reusable across fields. Faculty leave. Grants expire. Semesters turn over. A curated commons preserves the intellectual labor, the pedagogical experiments, and the governance evolution so that institutional knowledge persists beyond any single individual's tenure.

11. The Interdisciplinary Competency Gap Demands Institutional Response

Oudenampsen et al. (2024), conducting a systematic review of interdisciplinary learning outcomes in higher education for The Review of Higher Education, found that while the evidence for interdisciplinary learning benefits is growing, institutions still lack systematic approaches to making interdisciplinary resources and outcomes visible. A commons is one such systematic approach—it doesn't just store resources, it makes the connections between them discoverable.

12. Open Resources Accelerate Responsible AI Adoption

Gómez-Puente et al. (2024), studying AI in higher education through a systematic literature review in Frontiers in Education, found that AI's transformative potential is evident across multiple disciplines but that its responsible adoption requires shared frameworks, ethical guidelines, and collaborative approaches. An openly-licensed commons of AI resources—from governance documents to classroom applications—provides exactly this shared infrastructure, enabling institutions to move from ad hoc AI experimentation to coordinated, responsible integration.

The Network Effect at Lewis

The mechanisms described above converge in the AI Commons. When a nursing simulation is visible to a physics professor, lateral inspiration becomes possible. When a university AI policy is discoverable alongside its college-level adaptations and department-specific protocols, governance transparency becomes real. When interdisciplinary resources are tagged across every discipline they touch, the boundaries between programs become bridges.

This is not a repository. It is a culture—one that reflects the Lasallian tradition of education through association, made possible by the affordances of open digital infrastructure and the cross-disciplinary reach of artificial intelligence.
